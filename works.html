🔝 🔗 Publications

Various papers and publications I've written. ...well, there's only one so far.

Bit-Complexity of Classical Solutions of Linear Evolutionary Systems of Partial Differential Equations

Ivan Koswara, Gleb Pogudin, Svetlana Selivanova, Martin Ziegler
Journal of Complexity, Vol. 76, 2023.

As part of my Masters degree research, I worked on a specific problem. It's matrix multiplication/exponentiation, but with two specific criteria: the matrix is huge (formally, exponential-space; the input is a black box algorithm that can spit out any given entry), and the entries are real numbers (and so not precise; formally, that black box also accepts a precision argument and gives an approximation of the entry with that precision). It's not surprising that the matrix exponentiation problem with this setting is PSPACE-hard.

...or well, that's the most general formulation of the problem. The matrix has a lot of structure, since this problem really comes from trying to approximate solutions to partial differential equations. (The gist is you're sampling points at regular interval; then the continuous nature of a PDE becomes discrete, and a computer can approximate it.) When I brought certain structures to the matrix, the complexity of the problem went down. One particular result is, if the matrix is a circular band matrix with a fixed constant bandwidth, the matrix exponentiation problem falls into the complexity class #P (or its equivalent with real computation).

This complexity theory part of the research became my Masters thesis. The cited paper above talks more broadly with all the PDE setting.

Penalty theory for dynasty genres (updated: 17 November 2020)
In paper puzzles, "dynasty" is a term to refer to the ruleset "shade some cells black; no two black cells are orthogonally adjacent and all white cells form a contiguous region". Turns out there is a mathematical theory to count the maximum possible number of black cells in the grid; moreover, if a puzzle says the number of black cells has to be close to the maximum, then the theory can also infer a whole lot of things. I believe this theory was originally found by @agnomy on Twitter; I then translated it from Japanese into a tweet thread, and then wrote down a clearer (but still rough) version as a webpage above. Others have since taken the reins; for example, tckmn has this page that better introduces the theory. It's unlikely I will update this page any more, since better sources exist elsewhere; if any, I might take it down some time.

🔝 🔗 PuzzleScript

PuzzleScript is a scripting language to easily write a certain style of games: generally two-dimensional, grid-based, turn-based, and the rules generally just involve pattern matching. Many puzzle games (e.g. Sokoban) fall here, hence why the language is named so. Early on, I was very captivated with this language and wrote a few games in it. These are pretty old; I still have some ideas, but what I don't have is time and motivation.

12345 (2014)
My first ever game. The core concept is about merging blocks together; if a block tries to push into another like one, they merge and increase in number. There are several more mechanics later on. I've since learned games where you move everything on the grid at once feels clunky.
Repel (2014)
In the Google Group about PuzzleScript, I once saw someone sharing a game involving magnetic blocks that repel each other. I remember the level design was bad; a lot of space, a lot of boring block-pushing, and not enough of the fun mechanic. So I made my own take on it with several levels and a couple new mechanics. I think the new levels were somewhat better, but the mechanics were weird. (You can push a magnet against a solid object to "knock" the magnet, disabling it momentarily. What?)
Rock, Paper, Scissors (2015)
Perhaps the game I'm most proud of. Rock, paper, scissors; three kinds of objects, three different behaviors, with various interactions as objects interact. (Not too hard to guess what would happen, honestly.) While I'm very happy with the mechanics, I wish I had better level design. Maybe something to come back to.
JAM3 Game (2015)
A match-3 game, but somehow it's puzzly. Set up the board so that you can get a specific desired match. Apparently I made this in a few days, as something I could try to stream (because streaming writing puzzles would defeat the purpose of puzzles). Again, I think the core idea is interesting, but it might need some more whipping to shape.

Tetrobot and Co. (website)

A puzzle game where you control a robot that can transport and utilize various kinds of blocks to make your way through.

It has built-in Steam Workshop support. I really liked the idea of the game and had several ideas for custom levels. I was testing around with things and made these levels:

  • Buildbot: Build giant structures from a bunch of boxes.
  • Nine Problems: Solve nine minipuzzles.

After that, I noticed that many main-game levels were essentially divided into parts that otherwise didn't interact. In a way, the main-game levels felt not cohesive enough. What if I made them interact more? I also had this mischievous idea of making "alternate" versions of levels that utilized much more mechanics of the game; for example, levels that looked like early levels but needed knowledge of all the late-game mechanics. That results in these levels:

  • 1-2?: A Bot and His (Pesky) Block
  • 1-3?: Hopes Floating Away

Super Mario Maker 2

I did make a level for this, too! It's called "Back and Forth" (R8H-174-52G). True to my puzzle nature, you can look at the entire level from the start and there's nothing hidden (the breakable blocks don't hide anything, for example). I think this is a nice contrast from many other Mario levels. When every single RNG or unknown information is removed, can you put your skill to the test and beat the level?
