This page serves as an archive of my logic puzzles that I can recall writing.

Puzzles are organized by genre; under each heading, they are further organized by variant, then release date. Each genre has the rules for it marked in blue box (at the genre's heading for the general rules, under each individual puzzle for variant rules), but you can also search online (such as Puzzlink or Eric Fox's rules document) for other phrasing of the rules.

Each image is clickable and leads to a Penpa+ link, which you can use to solve the puzzle online; some puzzles also have a Puzzlink link below it if you prefer. (Note that Penpa+ links are very long and I didn't bother shortening them.)

I wrote several logic contests. The puzzles are organized by genre, but look at the sets section for links to the puzzles organized by set.

Non-logic puzzles are not included here; check my works page for a list of my puzzles. However, some hunt puzzles I wrote are essentially a collection of logic puzzles. They are still not listed here as they are not "pure" logic puzzles (they generally require you to find an "aha moment", some trick that is not explained in the rules), but you can find links to them in the hunt puzzles section.

Sometimes I may have forgotten I've written a certain puzzle (it's been over 10 years); feel free to contact me if you see something missing! Other times I've decided a puzzle is no longer worth including, because I'm ashamed of it. This very rarely happens, but it's possible. You can still contact me if you see a puzzle missing; if it falls into this "embarrassing" category, I'll just say so.

🔝 🔗 Cave

Draw a loop along the gridlines. The loop may not touch or cross itself. All numbers must be inside the loop. A given number indicates the number of cells "visible" from the given, including the cell itself. Here, a cell can see another if it's in the same row/column and the line of sight doesn't cross the loop.

Instead of drawing the loop, you can shade all cells outside of the loop.

Also known as Bag and Corral among other names (not to be confused with Coral, which is an entirely different genre). Although, nowadays, I think Cave is the name that sticks the most.

Divide the grid into dominoes. This way, each number is contained in a domino. A number indicates how many dominoes share a side (not just a corner) with the domino containing it. Dominoes may contain any number of given numbers.

🔝 🔗 Fillomino

🔝 🔗 Haisu

Draw a directed path connecting cell centers from S (start) to G (goal). The path must visit all cells. The grid is divided into regions, separated by thick borders. A number N indicates the path must be on the N-th visit to the region when it passes the number.

🔝 🔗 [2020-05-23] Haisu

In the Puzzlers Club server, there are events known as Logic Showcase, where people design logic puzzles to a given prompt, and then people vote for their favorite. One of the showcases was to write a Haisu puzzle, so I wrote this one. I intentionally kept it pretty simple; I knew Haisu was a mind-boggling genre and it was very easy to end up with something very difficult. (Even so, this puzzle is far from trivial; it takes some work, although no excessive lookahead.) Instead, I aimed for aesthetics, especially since the asymmetry between S and G meant even symmetric givens would resolve differently. I won the showcase with this entry. I personally think about half of the 13 entries went way overboard — one of them was a 29x29 that had a 107 on the grid — and appealing to the easier side of the masses gave me the votes.

🔝 🔗 Heyawake

Shade some cells black. Black cells may not be adjacent. The remaining white cells must form a contiguous area. No horizontal/vertical segment of white cells may span over two or more thick borders. A region (outlined by thick borders) with a number must contain the indicated number of black cells.

Heyawacky: There is literally no change from the above rules.

Some clarification might be in order. (The following is known to many regular logic puzzlers, but I write this down for posterity anyway.) Heyawake, as Nikoli defined it, uses only rectangular regions. People noticed this doesn't have to be the case. The main issue is with the rule about white segments. Nikoli defined it as "no white segment may span more than two rooms". This is equivalent to the above formulation, "no white segment may span over two thick borders", as long as the regions are rectangular. (Technically, as long as the regions are all orthogonally convex.) Once you have a region that is not convex, though, you might have a white segment that goes out of that region and then enters it again. Does it violate the rule? The logic puzzle community decides that it does, therefore using the thick border formulation. This also allows thick borders to exist independently, not just as region borders.

Because the regions can now be wacky, the genre is called Heyawacky. But after some time, people are tired of calling it such; instead, they simply treat Heyawake as if the genre had allowed wacky regions in the first place. For this archive, I'm going to do the same; although some of these puzzles should have been properly called Heyawacky, I'll just call them Heyawake.

🔝 🔗 [2020-05-09] Heyawake

I read up on a Twitter thread by @agnomy, which detailed a new theory in Heyawake. I wrote up my own version in English, with this puzzle accompanying it. It started a whole new kind of Heyawake puzzles (known as "penalty Heyawake" puzzles), together with more people writing up more approachable introduction to these: (1), (2), (3).

Some time before this, I had also introduced a kind of penalty theory for Nurikabe (although it's less used). Some time after penalty Heyawake, I also vaguely heard about something called river theory for Heyawake. The common thread to all of these is that they are all based on deep mathematical theory embedded in logic puzzles; nowadays, there are several enthusiasts for these kinds of puzzles, with some authors writing about them.

🔝 🔗 Hidoku

Draw a directed path connecting cell centers. The path must pass through all white cells. The cells of the path are numbered 1, 2, 3, ... from the starting cell (the range of used numbers is helpfully given to you, so you don't have to count the white cells). A number in a cell must coincide with the numbering of the path.

This genre is also known as Hidato, but that name is trademarked.

Some formulations of the rules require the smallest and the largest numbers to be given. I don't think that's necessary; removing that design constraint can lead to richer puzzles.

🔝 🔗 [2011-04-26] Minesweeper (Sudoku)

Variant rules: Minesweeper rules. Also, each row, column, and region contains the indicated number of mines.

I'm not sure how I came up with the idea of this genre. 🔝 🔗 Nurikabe

Blacken some cells to form a "wall": all black cells are contiguous through orthogonal adjacency, but there is no 2x2 square that is entirely black. Numbers may not be blackened. Each contiguous white area must contain exactly one number, which is equal to its area.

🔝 🔗 [2019-05-19] Nurikabe

I discovered a theory in Nurikabe puzzles, and I wanted to show it off using a toy example. This is the puzzle I decided to write for that. It can be easily solved intuitively, but I believe you need the theory in order to prove the solution is unique.

Click here to read the theory

Look at the gridpoints that touch some island. All internal gridpoints must touch islands, otherwise we have a 2x2 black area. However, an island of size N can touch at most 2N+2 gridpoints, so by summing up everything on the board, we know the maximum number of gridpoints touching islands. If this number is equal to, or close to, the number of internal gridpoints, then most of those gridpoints must be internal.

🔝 🔗 [2011-04-24] Nurikabe (No Cycle)

Variant rules: Nurikabe rules. In addition, not only there should be no 2x2 area of black cells, but also there is no cycle of black cells anywhere. There is also no cycle of white cells anywhere, including 2x2 areas.

I was deliberating on whether I should archive this puzzle or just skip it (under the "embarrassing puzzle" clause). I decided I would publish only this one and skip any future puzzle of this type. (That said, I think there is no other instance of this genre on my blogs.)

You might be wondering why there's all this strange rule about no cycles. I didn't know either! My very first exposure to Nurikabe was from a book in which it introduced the rules as above. I was actually confused when I learned the actual rules of Nurikabe, and for a while I resisted the change (including making this puzzle that would be ambiguous by normal Nurikabe rules). Over time I learned better, though, and now I follow the usual rules of Nurikabe. This puzzle serves simply as a look back to my history getting into logic puzzles.

Variant rules: Slitherlink rules. No 2x2 area can be entirely inside the loop. (It is fine for it a 2x2 area to be entirely outside the loop.)

🔝 🔗 [2023-11-26] Rampage

Divide the grid into dominoes. Each number represents a rampaging bull, which behaves as follows. The bull first moves to the other cell of the domino it's in, and then moves one extra space (thus breaking through the opposite short edge into a new domino). It then repeats this procedure with the new domino it's in, and so on. It can be shown the bull will eventually leave the grid; the number indicates how many dominoes the bull passes through, including the initial one. The following is an example of the behavior of two bulls. (Note that, as a puzzle, it might not be unique; it's only designed to explain the rules.)

I decided I would try my hand at making another genre that was interesting and simple. Then I remembered a math problem based on this procedure (specifically, that the bull will always leave the grid as long as the grid has no holes inside). It's very complicated to write in the rules, but I found the emergent behavior truly fascinating.

You will need to discover a lot of theory about this genre first, but I think it's worth the journey and it will be rewarding. Good luck!

Whether I want to write more puzzles of this genre: maybe so, there seem to be a lot of other techniques that I haven't discovered with this example puzzle. At the same time, having such a high barrier of entry (learning about the theory) might not be easy.

🔝 🔗 [2023-08-26] Star Battle (Regionless)

Place some stars on the grid, so each row/column has the indicated number of stars. Stars must be on cells that make up the grid. Stars may not touch, even diagonally.

A few days before this puzzle, there was a discussion in Puzzlers Club about 9x9 Regionless Star Battle. They can lead to visually striking puzzles. There might be only a small number of cells taken out, giving the impression that there are few clues and that it's difficult to break into, and yet the puzzle ends up unique and solves very nicely anyway.

I decided to try my hand on it, and this is the puzzle I wrote. The theme is that "the grid is torn". I'm sad I need an extra empty cell at the top-right, but I'm still very happy with this puzzle, especially since I think it's has a pretty unusual solve path for a Star Battle.

🔝 🔗 [2020-07-17] Starry Night

Fill some cells with a star, a sun, or a moon. Each row/column must contain exactly one of each symbol. Same symbols may not touch, even diagonally. Clues outside the grid describe which of the sun and moon is closer to the star in that row/column: a sun or a moon indicates that symbol is strictly closer to the star than the other, while a star indicates that the sun and the moon are equally close to the star.

Note: For the Penpa+ link, use */S/M instead of the star, sun, moon symbols to trigger the answer check. Did you know Penpa+ cannot check for sun and moon symbols?

Part of Typed Logic, a LMI contest in July 2020 written by Puzzlers Club members.

I think this genre doesn't have too much potential. First, it is easy to brute force with a computer. The number of ways to place one star in each row/column so that no two stars touch diagonally has been analyzed; for a 6x6 grid, that number is 90. Therefore, if we take stars, suns, and moons independently, we have 903 grids on 6x6. The number of valid grids is even smaller, since in many of these grids, two symbols will occupy the same cell. A computer can easily enumerate all of them; for each, it checks if the row/column clues are satisfied. Second, the number of grids is rather small, which means it's easy to put in just a few clues and make the puzzle unique. However, a clue by itself doesn't give much information. I believe this makes it very easy to set unfair puzzles. Third, the genre doesn't quite scale. This is a similar problem to the Skyscrapers genre. Enlarging the grid makes clues weaker, but you don't have many spaces to put clues.

That said, a little of this genre at a time can be fun. For all the above reasons, the genre is also obscure and unfamiliar, so when one appears, it feels fresh and new. When we were setting the contest, we had a problem filling in the Fairy-type slot; I went through some lists of puzzle genres, found this, and decided to go for it. This would end up being my only contribution to the contest, primarily because I was focusing on 25 Years, my own LMI contest, four months later. (On the other hand, it would be strange if I, the one coordinating the writing, didn't have anything in the contest itself.)

According to the discussion thread of the contest, this puzzle was "computer-assisted". As I said, there weren't many grids at all to go through, so I coded a solver. I then threw several pretty designs of the givens to the solver; if the solver reported exactly one solution, I tested the puzzle by hand to see if the logical path was reasonable. This is the resulting puzzle, which admittedly is incredibly pretty aesthetically. Its logical path suffers somewhat, though, detailed below.

I recognized a particular trait appearing in a few of my puzzles, which I call "disambiguate later". Some puzzles appear to be symmetrical, but the solution naturally doesn't have symmetry. However, it's not clear which way to break the symmetry. So, you just try one; if a later clue ends up breaking this, you mentally rotate/flip your solution (or rather, the remaining clues). This appears in this puzzle; if you ignore the moon clues, you end up having just a few solutions that are just reflections of each other, and you use the moon clues to pick one of these. I've learned that it feels yucky and off-putting; it doesn't quite feel logical, despite being completely legal. Now, if I set any such puzzle, I make sure to disambiguate earlier in the process instead of at the very end.

🔝 🔗 [2023-12-02] Turnaround

Draw a loop on cells. The loop must pass all numbers. Each number indicates how many turns the loop makes among the three cells: the number itself, and the cells immediately before and after it in the loop.

Note: For this puzzle, ignore the shading; it's for theme only.

I was thinking up of new genres. I made Contact and Rampage, both of which were region division puzzles, so I tried thinking of other things. I came up with the idea of loops. Personally, I find the rules for loops a bit inelegant; it takes some time to explain the loop can only travel orthogonally, cannot cross, and so on. (Although it's easier than explaining the rules for a wall like Nurikabe and Tapa.) So I looked for something particularly simple to complement this.

I then thought of Masyu, and how its clues depended on turns on the cell with the clue, as well as cells immediately before and after it. What if I made a different clue type for it? Counting turns was the most straightforward, and the genre came out that way.

The puzzle itself appeared to have a lot of freedom; I could experiment around as I built the puzzle. So I decided to go for a theme; not only the locations of the givens would be rotationally symmetric, but also there would be some other theme, in this case the date I posted this puzzle. And there it goes.
