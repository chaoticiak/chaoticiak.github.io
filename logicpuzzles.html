This page serves as an archive of my logic puzzles that I can recall writing. Only logic puzzles are here; non-logic puzzles are much less common and I've generally put them on my works page.

Puzzles are organized by genre, then by release date. Each genre has the rules for it, but you can also search online (such as Puzzlink or Eric Fox's rules document) for other phrasing of the rules. Each image is clickable and leads to a Penpa+ link, which you can use to solve the puzzle online; some puzzles also have a Puzzlink link below it if you prefer. (Note that Penpa+ links are very long and I didn't bother shortening them.)

Sometimes I may have forgotten I've written a certain puzzle (it's been over 10 years); feel free to contact me if you see something missing! Other times I've decided a puzzle is no longer worth including, because I'm ashamed of it. This very rarely happens, but it's possible. You can still contact me if you see a puzzle missing; if it falls into this "embarrassing" category, I'll just say so.

This page is long and has a lot of images. Be warned. It might be useful to search for something using your browser's search function. You can also click 🔗 next to a heading to get a link directly to that heading.

🔗 Akari

Place some lightbulbs on white cells. A lightbulb shines in the four cardinal directions, illuminating every white cell in its direction until hitting a black cell or the edge of the grid. Every empty white cell must be illuminated by some lightbulb. No lightbulb may illuminate another lightbulb. A number on a black cell indicates the number of lightbulbs that are orthogonally adjacent to the cell.

Mirror Akari: A cell that is diagonally split has a mirror along the diagonal. Illumination from lightbulbs is reflected along the diagonal, thus illuminating further cells in a different direction. Lightbulbs cannot be placed on cells with mirrors, but each white half still has to be illuminated. A lightbulb cannot illuminate itself either (e.g. by its illumination reflecting along mirrors in a cycle).

🔗 [2011-03-05] Akari

Second puzzle of my first blog. The reason the grid is not square was because when I wrote this puzzle (on paper), I miscounted the size of the grid, and then I didn't bother changing it. In my defense, I was still very new to puzzle-writing; I would have reworked it today.

Variant rules: Standard Mirror Akari rules (as given above). Just to emphasize, the double-sided mirrors need to be illuminated on both sides.

🔗 Fillomino

Divide the grid into regions. Two regions with the same area may not be orthogonally adjacent (they may touch at a corner). Each number must belong in a region with area equal to the number. Regions may have any number of given numbers (including none).

🔗 [2022-09-04] Fillomino

Published in Puzzler's Bulletin 17, a weekly series of puzzles curated by Tawan Sunathvanichkul as part of his TamBox series.

Tawan contacted me on whether I wanted to write a puzzle for his series. At this time, I was quite rusty and rarely wrote any puzzle, so I decided to say yes just so I had more practice. Since Fillomino was my favorite genre, I decided to write one. Initially I only put the givens in those locations without the additional aesthetic theme. Midway through writing, I noticed I had several rings of 1-6, and decided to extend it as the theme for the whole puzzle. It might not have been the wisest idea; the ending became somewhat unwieldy. Despite that, I'm still very satisfied with the puzzle.

Make sure to check out the TamBox series (whether Puzzler's Bulletins or the paid PULZE issues) for more good puzzles! (I haven't written any more for the series, though.)

🔗 Number in Order

Put a number in the given range into each white cell. Every "word" (a contiguous segment of white cells in a row/column, bounded by either black cells or the edge of the grid) must contain numbers that are consecutive, although not necessarily in order. (For example, 4-6-5 is okay, but 2-4-6 is not.)

🔗 [2011-03-05] Number in Order

The first genre I invented, although I think this itself might have been inspired from Str8ts, a genre that I now loathe because I got into an argument with its author in another Number in Order puzzle. At this point I still haven't learned how to set a good aesthetic theme or explore a new genre, but there are some neat little tricks.

🔗 Nurikabe

Blacken some cells to form a "wall": all black cells are contiguous through orthogonal adjacency, but there is no 2x2 square that is entirely black. Numbers may not be blackened. Each contiguous white area must contain exactly one number, which is equal to its area.

🔗 [2011-03-18] Nurikabe

More fun fact, this puzzle wasn't the first version. The first version had a 3 on R3C3. It was unique that way, but when I ported this puzzle to my second blog, I realized I could change that 3 into a 4 to give it a stronger aesthetic theme.

🔗 Slitherlink

Draw a loop along the gridpoints. The loop may only travel orthogonally and turn on gridpoints. It cannot cross or touch itself. A number on a cell indicates the number of segments of its cell that are part of the loop.

First puzzle I've ever written and published, on my first blog. That said, this was published simultaneously with a few other puzzles, so was it really the first?

🔗 [2011-03-22] Slitherlink (Star Battle)

Variant rules: Slitherlink rules. In addition, place some stars in the grid, so each row/column has the indicated number of stars. Stars must be inside the loop but not on given numbers. Unlike what the name "Star Battle" may imply, stars may touch each other.

The puzzle barely uses the Star Battle variant, which I also recognized back then. I would say this variant rule was a "patch" — something that was added strictly for uniqueness — but I think my past self actually thought this use of the variant was okay. Nowadays I would use the variant rule much more strongly, something like Serkan's puzzle in 2021. Also, the weird-looking rule that stars may touch each other is because this wasn't called "Star Battle" back then.
